The Corgi People Behind This Site

Corgi Hub exists because corgi people understand something that outsiders don't quite get: these dogs are not decorative. They are small dogs with working-dog brains, herding instincts, and opinions about your schedule. Writing good guides for corgi owners means writing for people who already know this.

Corgi People Understand

The Corgi Hub Writing Desk writes for an audience that already knows the basics. We do not explain that corgis are "smart" as though that is a revelation — our readers are aware, because their corgi has already reorganized the furniture to their own satisfaction. Our job is to go beyond the obvious and into the actually useful.

That means writing about herding behavior honestly — what it looks like when it's directed at children or cats, how to redirect it without crushing the dog's enthusiasm. It means covering ear development in detail, because new Pembroke owners spend a lot of time wondering why one ear is up and the other isn't. It means addressing the exercise paradox: corgis need more movement than their size implies, but they are also prone to weight gain if overindulged.

How We Work

The Corgi Hub Writing Desk is a small editorial team. We collaborate on research, challenge each other's drafts, and review everything before it publishes. We do not manufacture individual author personas or invented credentials — the content stands or falls on its accuracy and usefulness, not on who signed it.

We also try to take the breed's quirks seriously rather than just playing them for charm. Yes, corgi zoomies are delightful. They are also worth understanding from a behavior standpoint. Yes, the ears are famous. The timeline for when they stand up is genuinely variable, and owners deserve a real explanation.

What We Cover

  • Herding instinct and behavior management in household settings
  • Ear development — the honest timeline, what affects it, when to worry
  • Exercise and enrichment for a high-drive small dog
  • Shedding — Pembrokes shed significantly, and we are straightforward about it
  • Stair access, back health, and appropriate activity for a long-bodied breed
  • Training approaches suited to an opinionated, independent-thinking dog
