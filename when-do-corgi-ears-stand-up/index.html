When do corgi ears stand up? There is no single exact week that fits every puppy, but the evidence points consistently to a window of roughly 8 to 16 weeks as when most owners first see lift. Some ears come up earlier, some go up one at a time, and some make clear progress only to soften again during teething before settling later.

Why Owners Care About Corgi Ear Development So Much

The easiest place to start is the breed standard itself. The Pembroke Welsh Corgi Club of America breed standard calls for ears that are erect, firm, and medium in size, tapering slightly to a rounded point. That standard is one reason owners watch ear development so closely — the breed expectation is built in from the start.

But breed standards describe the end state, not the minute-by-minute puppy timeline. That gap is exactly where confusion begins. People see adult corgis with upright ears, then assume puppies should lock into that look on a fixed schedule. Real puppy development is messier than that. Understanding the actual biology of ear cartilage development makes the variation far less alarming.

Corgi Ear Development Week by Week: A Practical Timeline Reference

While no official "all corgi ears stand by week X" document exists, combining the breed standard, puppy development research, and consistent owner reports across forums and Reddit threads builds a useful working reference.

This table is a reference, not a strict rule. Individual variation within this window is entirely normal according to Eden Animal Hospital's puppy ear development guide, which notes that ears may partially lift around three to five months and settle as late as seven months for some breeds.

The Biology Behind Corgi Ear Development

Corgi ears are made primarily of auricular cartilage — the same type of cartilage that gives the human ear its shape. In newborn puppies, this cartilage is still soft and pliant. It hardens gradually over the first months of life as calcium deposits increase and the fibrous matrix of the cartilage matures.

The speed of this process varies between individual puppies and is influenced by genetics, nutrition, and physical development. Puppies from lines that historically produce consistently erect ears may show lift earlier. Puppies with slightly larger ear pinnae relative to their head size may take longer because there is more cartilage to stiffen. Neither scenario indicates a health problem — they are reflections of normal biological variation.

The muscles supporting ear posture also play a role. Auricular muscles attach to the base of the ear and the skull, and they strengthen gradually in step with overall puppy muscular development. A puppy whose ear muscles are still developing may show inconsistent ear carriage throughout the day — ears up in the morning, flopped by afternoon — simply because the muscles fatigue before they have fully matured.

Why Teething Gets Blamed So Often

Because owners keep seeing the same pattern. The live Reddit threads around this topic are unusually consistent: a puppy reaches the 3-to-5-month zone, one or both ears had started to perk, and then a droop appears again while the pup is teething.

The AKC puppy teething timeline explains that the most intensive teething phase — when adult teeth are pushing through and the jaw is working hardest — runs from approximately 12 to 20 weeks. This overlaps exactly with the stage where owners most commonly report ear wobble.

The most widely accepted explanation is that calcium and phosphorus resources get temporarily redirected toward bone and tooth development during heavy teething, which means slightly less available for ear cartilage development in that window. This is not a proven mechanism in the veterinary literature for corgis specifically, but it is consistent with the owner data and aligns with known physiology. Important distinction: "teething can make things wobble" is not the same as "ignore everything." If the ear looks painful, inflamed, or infected, stop treating it as a cute phase and talk to your vet.

When One Corgi Ear Stands Before the Other

One ear up and one ear down is one of the most common searches from corgi owners, and it is also one of the most consistently normal findings. Bilateral symmetry in puppy development is the exception, not the rule. The two ears do not receive the same mechanical stimulation (dogs turn their heads differently in each direction), they do not necessarily have identical cartilage composition, and the auricular muscles on each side develop at slightly different rates.

Owner reports from r/corgi overwhelmingly describe this as a phase that resolves on its own. Puppies with one ear still down at 12 weeks who have the other fully erect are rarely the same puppies still showing asymmetry at 20 weeks. The data is anecdotal, but it is consistent enough to be reassuring.

The scenarios that are less expected — and worth watching — are: an ear that had been consistently upright then suddenly dropped with no teething context, or asymmetry that worsens rather than gradually improving past 5 months.

What Is More Useful Than Watching the Calendar

The smartest question is not "why isn't this perfect yet?" It is "does this still look like normal development?" That shifts your attention to pattern rather than impatience. For most puppies, normal development means change over time, not identical progress every week.

Useful things to watch instead of obsessing over the specific week:

  • Is there gradual change over the months — even if it is not linear?
  • Is one ear experimenting with lift, even if it does not hold position yet?
  • Did the droop appear during the heavy chewing and teething stage?
  • Does the ear look healthy and comfortable, with no redness, odour, or discharge?

If the answer to the first three is mostly yes and the fourth is also yes, waiting is almost always more productive than intervening. Should You Tape a Corgi Puppy's Ears?

This is where internet advice gets noisy fast. Some owners jump straight to taping because they are afraid of "missing the window." Others treat any mention of taping like a scam. The better answer is more restrained: do not make taping your default move just because a young puppy's ears are not perfect yet.

Because the timeline naturally varies, many puppies simply need more time. If you are seriously considering taping, get guidance from someone who knows the breed well — ideally your breeder or veterinarian — instead of improvising with random household materials. The risky part is not just the tape itself. It is the false urgency that makes owners act before they understand what is actually normal.

Taping is most commonly discussed as a last resort when a puppy has consistently floppy ears past 6 months and cartilage has not set. Before that point, most experienced corgi breeders recommend watchful waiting over intervention.

When to Worry About Health Rather Than Timeline

The biggest mistake owners make is worrying about the wrong thing. Slow ears are usually less concerning than painful ears. If the ear posture is odd but the ear looks healthy, that often points back to development. If the posture changes alongside other symptoms, the conversation changes completely.

According to VCA Animal Hospitals' guide to ear infections in dogs, key warning signs that distinguish health problems from normal development include: head shaking, repeated scratching at the ear, visible redness or swelling inside the ear canal, discharge or an unusual odour, and pain when the ear base is touched. Sudden change in ear posture after a possible head injury is also a separate category that warrants immediate attention.

These are not development questions. These are health questions that need a vet, not a timeline article.

The Most Honest Answer to "When Do Corgi Ears Stand Up?"

If you want the shortest version after all that nuance: many corgi ears begin to stand somewhere during the first few months, many go through awkward teething-era reversals, and some simply take longer than owners expect. The breed standard explains the destination. Biology and individual variation explain the detours.

That is why the best answer is not a neat "12 weeks exactly" claim. The best answer is that variation is normal, especially before teething is finished, and the real red flags are pain, infection, or injury — not a puppy whose ears are still figuring themselves out. If you are approaching the 6-month mark and ears are still consistently down, that is the right time to have a conversation with your vet and your breeder.

FAQ

When do corgi ears stand up?

Most owners see the first signs of lift between 8 and 16 weeks, but the full process — including teething reversals — often runs to 5 or 6 months. There is no single universal week.

Is one ear up and one ear down normal?

Yes. Asymmetric ear development is one of the most common phases in corgi puppies and almost always resolves on its own before 6 months.

Can teething make a corgi ear droop again?

Yes. This is one of the most consistent owner reports from corgi communities. Ear posture often wobbles during the 12–20 week intensive teething period before settling again as adult teeth come in.

Should I tape my corgi's ears?

Not automatically. Many puppies simply need more time. If you are past 6 months and cartilage is still soft, talk to your breeder and vet before taping. Before that point, watchful waiting is almost always the right move.

What is the biology behind corgi ears standing up?

Auricular cartilage hardens gradually as calcium deposits increase and the fibrous cartilage matrix matures. The auricular muscles that hold ear posture also strengthen over the same period. Both processes are ongoing through the first 6 months of life.

Why does my corgi's ear look different morning vs. evening?

Auricular muscle fatigue is a real factor in puppies. Muscles that hold ear posture may hold upright position in the morning but flag by evening as the day progresses. This typically resolves as the muscles strengthen.

When should I stop waiting and call the vet about corgi ears?

Call if the ear is painful, red, smelly, swollen, discharging, or if posture changed suddenly after a possible injury. Slow ear development alone is not a vet-call reason. An ear that is still floppy at or past 6 months warrants a conversation with your vet and breeder.

Do corgi ear genetics matter?

Yes. Puppies from lines with a consistent history of early ear lift often develop earlier than average. Ear pinna size relative to head size also plays a role — larger pinnae take more cartilage development to stand. Neither factor is controllable after purchase, but both help explain variation between siblings.
